Adding a Spa or Hot Tub to Your Fiberglass Pool

A spa takes your pool experience to the next level, offering year-round relaxation and therapeutic benefits. Explore integrated spas, spillover designs, and standalone options for your Main Line backyard.

There's something magical about soaking in warm water on a cool Pennsylvania evening, steam rising as you gaze at the stars. A spa or hot tub transforms your pool area from a summer-only feature into a year-round retreat.

If you're planning a new fiberglass pool or considering upgrading an existing one, adding a spa is one of the most popular - and most rewarding - choices you can make.

Why Add a Spa to Your Pool?

Year-Round Use

In Pennsylvania's climate, your pool sees action roughly 4-5 months per year. A heated spa extends your outdoor water enjoyment to 12 months. Many spa owners find they use their hot tub even more in winter than summer.

Health and Wellness Benefits

  • Muscle relaxation: Warm water and jets soothe sore muscles
  • Stress relief: Hydrotherapy reduces cortisol and promotes relaxation
  • Better sleep: Soaking before bed improves sleep quality
  • Joint relief: Buoyancy reduces pressure on joints
  • Social connection: Spas naturally bring people together for conversation

Property Value

A pool with an integrated spa is more attractive to future buyers than a pool alone. The spa signals a complete outdoor living experience and can increase your home's appeal.

Understanding Your Options

You have three main approaches to adding a spa to your pool environment:

Integrated Spa

Part of Pool

Built into the fiberglass shell, shares water with pool

Spillover Spa

Elevated

Separate raised unit that cascades into the pool

Standalone

Independent

Separate hot tub adjacent to your pool

Option 1: Integrated Fiberglass Spa

An integrated spa is built directly into the fiberglass pool shell, typically positioned at one end of the pool. The spa section is usually raised slightly and separated by a small wall or spillover.

Integrated Spa Features

  • Part of the single fiberglass shell
  • Typically 6-8 feet across, 3-4 feet deep
  • Built-in seating for 4-8 people
  • Dedicated jets and blower system
  • Can be isolated for independent heating
+$15,000 - $25,000 to pool cost

Advantages

  • Seamless, cohesive design
  • Single installation process
  • Shared equipment reduces complexity
  • Easy to move between pool and spa
  • Lower total cost than separate units

Considerations

  • Must be planned during initial pool design
  • Harder to maintain different temperatures
  • Limited size and configuration options
  • Heating the spa warms the whole pool

Option 2: Spillover Spa

A spillover spa is a separate elevated unit (fiberglass or concrete) that's connected to the main pool by a waterfall or sheet of water that cascades from the spa into the pool.

Spillover Spa Features

  • Raised 12-24 inches above pool level
  • Completely separate water and heating system
  • Independent temperature control (104°F spa, 80°F pool)
  • Dramatic visual waterfall feature
  • Can operate when pool is closed for winter
+$20,000 - $40,000 to pool cost

Advantages

  • Independent temperature control
  • Year-round spa use even with pool closed
  • Beautiful waterfall visual/sound
  • More flexibility in placement
  • Can add to existing pools

Considerations

  • Higher cost than integrated
  • More complex equipment/plumbing
  • Requires stepping up to enter spa
  • More maintenance (two bodies of water)

Best of Both Worlds

A spillover spa gives you the visual connection of a single water feature while providing the practical benefits of separate systems. It's our most popular recommendation for homeowners who want both pool and spa experiences.

Option 3: Standalone Hot Tub

A standalone hot tub is a completely separate unit placed near (but not connected to) your pool. This is the most flexible option and can be added to any property, with or without a pool.

Standalone Hot Tub Features

  • Fully independent - its own plumbing, heating, cover
  • Portable (can be moved if needed)
  • Huge range of brands, sizes, and features
  • Professional jets and seating configurations
  • Available with advanced features (stereo, lights, waterfalls)
$8,000 - $20,000 + pad/electrical

Advantages

  • Most feature-rich option
  • Completely independent operation
  • Can add to existing pool at any time
  • Portable if you move
  • Wide range of price points

Considerations

  • Not visually integrated with pool
  • Requires separate pad and electrical
  • Different aesthetic than pool
  • No water feature connection

Key Considerations for Pennsylvania

Year-Round Use in Our Climate

If year-round spa use is important to you, consider:

  • Spillover or standalone for true independence
  • Quality cover to retain heat and reduce costs
  • Efficient heater (gas heats faster, electric costs less to operate)
  • Accessible location - you'll use it more if you don't have to trudge through snow

Electrical Requirements

Spas require significant electrical capacity:

  • Integrated spa: Usually 50-60 amp service
  • Spillover spa: Separate 50-60 amp circuit
  • Standalone: 220V dedicated circuit, 40-60 amps

Budget $1,500-$3,000 for electrical installation if running new service.

Placement and Privacy

Think about where you'll enjoy your spa most:

  • Privacy from neighbors (fencing, landscaping)
  • Views you want to enjoy while soaking
  • Proximity to house (closer = more winter use)
  • Access for service and maintenance

Making Your Decision

Choose Integrated If:

  • You're building a new pool anyway
  • Budget is a primary concern
  • You prefer seamless aesthetics
  • You'll mainly use the spa in warmer months

Choose Spillover If:

  • Year-round spa use is important
  • You want a dramatic water feature
  • Independent temperature control matters
  • You have budget for the upgrade

Choose Standalone If:

  • You already have a pool
  • Maximum jet/feature options are priority
  • You want portability
  • You prefer separation between pool and spa

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I add a spa to an existing fiberglass pool?

Adding an integrated spa to an existing fiberglass pool is very difficult and usually not recommended. However, you can add a standalone hot tub adjacent to your pool, or install a raised spillover spa that connects visually but has separate plumbing. Planning for a spa during initial pool installation is much more cost-effective.

How much does it cost to add a spa to a fiberglass pool?

An integrated fiberglass pool/spa combination adds $15,000-$25,000 to the base pool cost. A raised spillover spa typically costs $20,000-$40,000 additional. A standalone quality hot tub ranges from $8,000-$20,000 plus $2,000-$5,000 for pad and electrical installation.

What's the difference between an integrated spa and a spillover spa?

An integrated spa is part of the pool shell with shared water and equipment. A spillover spa is a separate elevated unit with its own heating and jets, connected to the pool by a waterfall that overflows into the main pool. Spillover spas offer independent temperature control and more dramatic visual effects.

Can I heat just the spa and not the whole pool?

With an integrated spa, you can typically isolate and heat just the spa section using diverter valves, though some heat transfer occurs. Spillover and standalone spas have completely independent heating, making them more efficient for spa-only use, especially for year-round enjoyment in Pennsylvania.

Are fiberglass spas as good as acrylic hot tubs?

Fiberglass spas are excellent when integrated with fiberglass pools, offering seamless aesthetics and shared equipment. Standalone acrylic hot tubs typically offer more jets, seats, and features per dollar. For a pool-adjacent spa experience, integrated fiberglass looks better; for standalone spa performance, acrylic often wins.
