Fiberglass Pool Maintenance: A Complete Guide

Fiberglass pools are the easiest to maintain of any pool type. Here's everything you need to know about keeping your pool clean, balanced, and beautiful year after year in Pennsylvania's climate.

Congratulations on your fiberglass pool! You've chosen the most maintenance-friendly pool type available. While all pools require some care, fiberglass pools demand significantly less time and money to keep pristine compared to concrete or vinyl alternatives.

This comprehensive guide covers everything you need to know about maintaining your fiberglass pool, from weekly routines to seasonal tasks and troubleshooting common issues.

Why Fiberglass Pools Are Easier to Maintain

Before diving into the maintenance routine, let's understand why fiberglass pools have earned their reputation for low maintenance:

  • Non-porous surface: The gelcoat finish is completely smooth, giving algae nothing to grip onto
  • Less chemical use: The inert surface doesn't affect water chemistry like concrete does
  • No brushing required: Unlike concrete's rough plaster, fiberglass rarely needs scrubbing
  • Resists staining: The gelcoat surface is resistant to most common pool stains
  • No resurfacing: The gelcoat lasts 25-30 years without major renovation

Time Comparison

Fiberglass: 15-20 minutes/week | Concrete: 45-60 minutes/week | Vinyl: 25-35 minutes/week

Weekly Maintenance Routine

15-20 minutes/week

Weekly Tasks

These tasks should be performed 1-2 times per week during swimming season:

  • Test water chemistry (pH, chlorine/bromine levels)
  • Empty skimmer baskets of leaves and debris
  • Check pump basket and clear if needed
  • Skim surface for floating debris
  • Check water level (maintain at mid-skimmer)
  • Inspect equipment for leaks or unusual sounds

Testing Water Chemistry

The most important weekly task is testing and adjusting your water chemistry. Proper balance protects your pool surface, keeps swimmers comfortable, and prevents algae growth.

What to Test Weekly:

  • pH: Should be 7.2-7.6
  • Free chlorine: Should be 1-3 ppm (or bromine 3-5 ppm)

Testing Methods:

  • Test strips: Quick and easy, accuracy varies
  • Liquid test kits: More accurate, requires more time
  • Digital testers: Most accurate, higher cost

Understanding Water Chemistry

Balanced water chemistry is the foundation of pool maintenance. Here are the key parameters and their ideal ranges for fiberglass pools:

pH Level

Test 2x/week

Free Chlorine

1 - 3 ppm
Test 2x/week

Total Alkalinity

80 - 120 ppm
Test monthly

Calcium Hardness

200 - 400 ppm
Test monthly

Why pH Matters

pH is the most important parameter to monitor. When pH drifts:

  • Too low (acidic): Corrodes equipment, irritates eyes/skin
  • Too high (basic): Chlorine becomes ineffective, scale forms

Fiberglass pH Tip

Fiberglass pools tend to have more stable pH than concrete pools because the gelcoat surface doesn't react with water the way plaster does. You'll likely need fewer pH adjustments.

Monthly Maintenance Tasks

30-45 minutes/month

Monthly Tasks

  • Test total alkalinity and calcium hardness
  • Clean waterline with pool-safe cleaner
  • Inspect filter and clean/backwash as needed
  • Check automatic cleaner (if applicable)
  • Lubricate O-rings and gaskets
  • Take water sample to pool store for comprehensive analysis

Filter Maintenance

Your filter type determines cleaning procedures:

  • Cartridge filter: Remove and hose off monthly, replace cartridge annually
  • Sand filter: Backwash when pressure rises 8-10 PSI above clean, replace sand every 5-7 years
  • DE filter: Backwash monthly, full breakdown and cleaning annually

Cleaning the Waterline

Oils, sunscreen, and environmental debris can create a ring at the waterline. For fiberglass pools:

  • Use a soft cloth or sponge (no abrasive pads)
  • Use enzyme-based cleaners or mild dish soap
  • Avoid harsh chemicals that could damage the gelcoat

Never Use These on Fiberglass

Avoid abrasive scrubbers, wire brushes, chlorine tablets directly on surfaces, or household cleaners not designed for pools. These can damage the gelcoat finish.

Seasonal Care: Pennsylvania Climate

Opening Your Pool (April-May)

  1. Remove and clean winter cover
  2. Remove winterizing plugs from returns and skimmers
  3. Reinstall pump, filter, and other equipment
  4. Fill pool to proper level
  5. Start pump and check for proper circulation
  6. Test and balance water chemistry
  7. Shock the pool
  8. Run filter continuously until water clears
  9. Install ladders, handrails, diving board

Mid-Season Care (June-August)

  • Maintain regular weekly and monthly routines
  • Run pump 8-12 hours daily during peak use
  • Shock after heavy use or rain storms
  • Monitor chlorine more carefully in hot weather (it depletes faster)
  • Check for algae growth, especially in shaded areas

Closing Your Pool (September-October)

  1. Balance water chemistry carefully
  2. Lower water level below skimmers (but never drain completely!)
  3. Blow out plumbing lines with air compressor
  4. Install winterizing plugs in return lines
  5. Add winterizing chemicals as directed
  6. Remove ladders, handrails, diving board
  7. Disconnect and drain pump, filter, heater
  8. Install winter cover securely

Never Drain a Fiberglass Pool

Unlike concrete pools, fiberglass pools should never be completely drained. The water provides counterbalance against groundwater pressure. An empty fiberglass pool can "float" or pop out of the ground, causing catastrophic damage.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Problem: Cloudy Water

Possible Causes:

  • Poor filtration (dirty filter, not running long enough)
  • Unbalanced chemistry (high pH, low chlorine)
  • High calcium hardness
  • Environmental factors (pollen, dust)

Solutions:

  • Clean or backwash filter, run 24 hours
  • Test and balance chemistry
  • Add clarifier to help filter fine particles
  • Shock the pool

Problem: Algae Growth

Possible Causes:

  • Low chlorine levels
  • Poor circulation in dead spots
  • High phosphate levels (algae food)

Solutions:

  • Shock with 3-4x normal chlorine dose
  • Brush affected areas (fiberglass rarely needs this)
  • Run pump 24 hours until clear
  • Add algaecide as preventive

Problem: Stains on Gelcoat

Types of Stains:

  • Metal stains: Blue-green (copper) or brown (iron)
  • Organic stains: Brown/green from leaves, algae
  • Scale: White deposits from calcium

Solutions:

  • Identify the stain type (vitamin C test: if stain lightens, it's metal)
  • Use appropriate stain remover for the type
  • Address source (metal sequestrant for metal, balance calcium)

DIY vs Professional Service

What You Can Easily Do Yourself:

  • Weekly testing and chemical adjustments
  • Skimming and cleaning baskets
  • Monthly waterline cleaning
  • Operating automatic cleaners

Consider Professional Help For:

  • Pool opening and closing (especially in cold climates)
  • Equipment repairs
  • Persistent water chemistry issues
  • Stain removal
  • Major equipment upgrades

Frequently Asked Questions

Are fiberglass pools hard to maintain?

Fiberglass pools are actually the easiest pool type to maintain. The smooth, non-porous gelcoat surface resists algae growth, requires less brushing than concrete, and uses fewer chemicals. Most owners spend 15-20 minutes per week on maintenance versus 45-60 minutes for concrete pools.

How often should I test my pool water?

Test pH and chlorine levels at least twice per week during swimming season. Test total alkalinity, calcium hardness, and cyanuric acid monthly. Take a water sample to a pool store for comprehensive analysis at the start of the season and monthly thereafter.

Do fiberglass pools need to be resurfaced?

Unlike concrete pools that need resurfacing every 10-15 years, fiberglass pools don't require resurfacing in the traditional sense. The gelcoat finish may need restoration after 15-25 years if it becomes faded or chalky, but this is less invasive and less expensive than concrete replastering.

Can I use a robotic cleaner on a fiberglass pool?

Yes, robotic cleaners work excellently on fiberglass pools. The smooth surface allows robots to navigate efficiently with less wear on their brushes. Choose models with rubber or soft brushes rather than aggressive scrubbing heads to protect the gelcoat finish.

What causes stains on fiberglass pools?

Common stains include metal stains (from copper pipes or well water) appearing as blue-green or brown, organic stains from leaves and debris, and calcium deposits from hard water. Most stains can be removed with the appropriate treatment. Maintaining proper chemistry prevents most staining issues.

How do I winterize a fiberglass pool in Pennsylvania?

Lower water level below skimmers (never drain completely), balance chemistry and add winterizing chemicals, blow out plumbing lines and plug them, remove and store equipment like ladders, install a winter cover, and run the pump briefly during extreme cold to prevent freezing. Many PA homeowners hire professionals for winterization.
