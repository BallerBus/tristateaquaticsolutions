Fiberglass Pool Specialists vs General Pool Contractors

If you've decided on a fiberglass pool, your next question is crucial: should you hire a fiberglass specialist or a general pool contractor who also installs fiberglass? The difference can be significant.

Fiberglass pools offer tremendous advantages: faster installation, lower maintenance, smooth surfaces, and structural warranties. But these benefits only materialize when the pool is installed correctly. And fiberglass installation is a specialized skill.

This guide explains why fiberglass expertise matters and how to identify installers who truly understand this pool type.

Why Fiberglass Specialization Matters

Fiberglass pool installation is fundamentally different from concrete pool construction. The techniques that make a great concrete builder don't automatically transfer to fiberglass. Here's why:

Different Skills, Different Risks

Concrete pool construction is about building a structure from the ground up - excavation, steel framework, gunite application, finishing, and curing. The builder creates the pool shell on-site.

Fiberglass installation is about handling and setting a pre-manufactured shell. The pool already exists - the installer's job is to:

  • Excavate to precise specifications
  • Prepare a perfectly level base
  • Transport and place a fragile shell without damage
  • Level the shell with exacting precision
  • Backfill without distorting the shell
  • Connect plumbing without compromising the structure

Each step has unique requirements that differ from concrete work.

The Precision Factor

A fiberglass pool shell is manufactured to precise specifications. If the installation is off by even a small margin, problems emerge: uneven water lines, stress fractures, plumbing issues, and premature wear. Specialists understand these tolerances.

Equipment and Training

Proper fiberglass installation requires:

  • Specialized lifting equipment: Crane or hydraulic lift systems designed for shell handling
  • Precision leveling tools: Laser levels and specialized measuring equipment
  • Specific backfill materials: Clean stone aggregate, not just any fill dirt
  • Manufacturer training: Understanding of specific brand requirements

Key Installation Differences

Excavation

Specialist approach: Excavates to precise dimensions based on the specific shell model, accounting for base material depth and exact shape contours.

General contractor approach: May dig a general hole and adjust, potentially leading to improvised solutions that compromise installation quality.

Base Preparation

Specialist approach: Creates a carefully graded and compacted base using appropriate materials (typically crushed stone or sand), leveled to manufacturer specifications.

General contractor approach: May use unsuitable materials or skip proper compaction, leading to settling and shell stress over time.

Shell Placement

Specialist approach: Uses proper lifting equipment with soft straps at designated lift points, placing shell in a controlled manner that prevents stress or damage.

General contractor approach: May use improper lifting methods that stress the shell or place it too quickly without proper alignment.

Leveling

Specialist approach: Uses laser levels and precision measurements, adjusting until the shell is within manufacturer tolerances (typically 1/4" or less).

General contractor approach: May accept "good enough" leveling that creates visible water lines and potential structural issues.

Backfilling

Specialist approach: Fills water and backfill simultaneously in controlled stages, using clean aggregate and compacting in lifts to prevent voids.

General contractor approach: May rush backfill process, use improper materials, or skip simultaneous water filling, risking shell distortion.

The Backfill Mistake

One of the most common errors by inexperienced installers is improper backfilling. If the shell is backfilled without water, or if water and backfill aren't balanced, the shell can flex or "float." This causes permanent damage that may not be obvious immediately but leads to problems later.

Certifications to Look For

Major fiberglass pool manufacturers offer certification programs for installers. These certifications indicate the installer has received manufacturer-specific training and meets their installation standards.

Why Certifications Matter

  • Training: Certified installers have completed manufacturer training on proper techniques
  • Warranty compliance: Some manufacturers require certified installation for full warranty coverage
  • Ongoing education: Certified dealers typically receive updates on new products and techniques
  • Accountability: Manufacturers track certified installer performance

Common Fiberglass Installation Mistakes

When fiberglass pools are installed by non-specialists, these problems commonly occur:

1

Improper Leveling

The pool appears "off" with visible water line differences. Can cause uneven stress on the shell and aesthetic problems that are virtually impossible to fix without removal.

2

Backfill Voids

Gaps form between the shell and surrounding material. Can cause shell movement, cracking, and plumbing failures. Often not discovered until significant damage occurs.

3

Shell Stress During Installation

Improper lifting or placement creates stress cracks or spider webbing in the gelcoat. May appear months or years later as the shell flexes under normal use.

4

Poor Base Preparation

Settling occurs after installation as improper base compacts. Leads to unlevel pool, potential shell damage, and cracked decking.

5

Plumbing Compromises

Connections made improperly or plumbing routed in ways that stress fittings. Leads to leaks that are difficult to access and repair.

Warranty Implications

Fiberglass pool warranties typically have two components: the manufacturer's structural warranty and the installer's workmanship warranty.

Manufacturer Warranty

Major manufacturers offer structural warranties of 25 years to lifetime on the shell itself. However, these warranties often have conditions:

  • Installation by an authorized/certified dealer
  • Compliance with manufacturer installation specifications
  • Proper water chemistry maintenance
  • Registration within specified timeframe

The Warranty Gap

If an uncertified installer makes an error that damages your pool, you may find yourself in a gray area where neither the manufacturer nor the installer accepts responsibility. The manufacturer may claim improper installation, while the installer may claim a defective shell.

Installer Workmanship Warranty

A good fiberglass specialist typically offers:

  • 2-5 year workmanship warranty on installation
  • Equipment warranties (pumps, heaters, etc.)
  • Plumbing warranty
  • Clear warranty transfer process if you sell the home

Questions to Ask Any Fiberglass Installer

About Experience

  • "How many fiberglass pools have you installed in the past year?"
  • "What percentage of your business is fiberglass vs. other pool types?"
  • "Which fiberglass manufacturers are you certified with?"

About Process

  • "Walk me through your installation process step by step."
  • "What material do you use for backfill?"
  • "How do you ensure the shell is properly leveled?"
  • "Do you fill water and backfill simultaneously? At what intervals?"

About Warranties

  • "Is the manufacturer warranty valid with your installation?"
  • "What does your workmanship warranty cover specifically?"
  • "How do you handle warranty claims?"

About Equipment

  • "What lifting equipment do you use for shell placement?"
  • "Do you own this equipment or rent it?"
  • "What happens if the shell is damaged during delivery or placement?"

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Can't name their fiberglass manufacturer certifications
  • Fiberglass is a small part of their business
  • Vague about backfill process and materials
  • Unwilling to provide fiberglass-specific references
  • Can't explain how they ensure proper leveling
  • Defensive about installation methodology questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Why should I choose a fiberglass specialist over a general contractor?

Fiberglass pool installation requires specific expertise in shell handling, leveling, backfill procedures, and plumbing connections. Specialists typically have manufacturer certifications, specialized equipment, and extensive experience that reduces installation errors and ensures valid warranties.

What certifications should a fiberglass pool installer have?

Look for installers certified by major fiberglass manufacturers like Latham, Thursday Pools, Leisure Pools, or San Juan Pools. These certifications require training on proper installation techniques and may be required for full warranty coverage.

Can a concrete pool builder install a fiberglass pool?

While some concrete specialists also install fiberglass, the techniques differ significantly. A builder primarily focused on concrete may lack the specific training and equipment for optimal fiberglass installation. Always verify their fiberglass-specific experience and certifications.

How do I verify a fiberglass installer's certification?

Ask the installer for their certification documentation and verify directly with the manufacturer. Most manufacturers have online dealer locators where certified installers are listed. If an installer claims certification but isn't on the manufacturer's list, ask why.

What warranty issues can arise from improper fiberglass installation?

Manufacturers may deny warranty claims if installation didn't follow their specifications. Common warranty-voiding issues include improper backfill, inadequate base preparation, shell damage during installation, and plumbing problems. A certified installer helps ensure your warranty remains valid.
