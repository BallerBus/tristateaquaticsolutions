Gunite Pool Problems: Why Homeowners Are Switching to Fiberglass

After years of dealing with cracking, algae, and expensive maintenance, many gunite pool owners are making a change. Here's why fiberglass is becoming the preferred choice for Pennsylvania homeowners.

For decades, gunite (concrete) pools were considered the gold standard of inground pools. They offered unlimited design flexibility and a certain prestige. But as pool technology has evolved and homeowners have gained more experience with these pools, the picture has become more nuanced.

Today, many gunite pool owners - especially in Pennsylvania's challenging climate - are increasingly frustrated with ongoing maintenance demands, unexpected repair costs, and the constant battle against algae. Let's examine the real issues and why fiberglass has emerged as a compelling alternative.

The Gunite Reality Check

We're not saying gunite pools are bad - they're not. In the right circumstances, with proper maintenance and realistic expectations, gunite pools can provide decades of enjoyment. But we believe homeowners deserve honest information before making a major investment.

The challenges we're about to discuss aren't manufacturing defects or poor construction - they're inherent characteristics of concrete pool technology that every gunite owner eventually faces.

Common Gunite Pool Problems

Problem #1: Surface Deterioration

Gunite pool surfaces are finished with plaster, pebble, or other aggregate materials. These surfaces are not permanent. Plaster typically lasts 10-15 years before needing replacement at a cost of $10,000-$20,000. Even premium pebble finishes degrade over time and eventually need refinishing.

Problem #2: Persistent Algae

The porous nature of concrete and plaster provides countless microscopic places for algae to establish. Once algae gets into the surface, it becomes a recurring battle. Gunite pool owners spend 2-3x more on algaecides and shock treatments than fiberglass owners, yet still deal with algae outbreaks.

Problem #3: Rough Surface

New plaster feels smooth, but it becomes rough within a few years as the surface etches from chemicals and age. Swimmers complain of scraped feet, knees, and hands. Children especially dislike the rough texture. Some owners resort to wearing water shoes in their own pools.

Problem #4: Cracking

Concrete is rigid and does not flex. Ground movement, temperature changes, and settling can cause cracks. While hairline cracks are often cosmetic, they can worsen and lead to structural problems or water loss. Crack repair is an ongoing maintenance item.

Problem #5: Staining

Plaster and pebble surfaces absorb minerals from water, creating stains that are difficult or impossible to remove without acid washing or replastering. Iron, copper, and calcium deposits leave permanent marks. Even with perfect chemistry, some staining is inevitable over time.

The Maintenance Burden

Gunite pools require significantly more ongoing maintenance than fiberglass. Here's what owners report spending each week:

Weekly Gunite Maintenance (45-60 minutes)

  • Brushing walls and floor (essential to prevent algae in porous surface)
  • Vacuuming (more debris sticks to rough surfaces)
  • Chemical testing and adjustment (more precise balance needed)
  • Skimmer basket cleaning
  • Filter maintenance (higher chemical use means more filter cleaning)

Weekly Fiberglass Maintenance (15-20 minutes)

  • Quick brush if needed (often not necessary)
  • Chemical testing and adjustment
  • Skimmer basket cleaning

This difference adds up to 40+ hours per year of additional maintenance time for gunite owners. That's a week of vacation spent maintaining your pool instead of enjoying it.

Pennsylvania Climate Challenges

Pennsylvania's climate is particularly hard on gunite pools.

Freeze-Thaw Cycles

The Main Line area experiences approximately 100 freeze-thaw cycles annually. Each cycle stresses rigid concrete structures:

  • Water in hairline cracks expands when frozen
  • Cracks widen with each freeze-thaw cycle
  • Tile lines are especially vulnerable to frost damage
  • Coping stones can lift and shift

Fiberglass Advantage

Fiberglass pools are designed to flex with temperature changes. The composite material expands and contracts without cracking. This is why fiberglass pools perform exceptionally well in northern climates while gunite pools struggle.

Winter Closing Concerns

Both pool types need winterization, but gunite pools face additional risks:

  • Plaster can crack if water level drops too low
  • Ice expansion against walls causes micro-damage
  • Spring opening often reveals new cracks or tile damage
  • Acid washing may be needed after winter to remove staining

The Fiberglass Alternative

Modern fiberglass pools address virtually every gunite complaint:

Smooth, Non-Porous Surface

  • Gelcoat surface remains smooth for the life of the pool
  • No rough texture to scrape skin
  • Algae cannot embed in the surface
  • Easier to clean - debris slides off

Structural Integrity

  • One-piece construction with no joints to fail
  • Flexes with ground movement and temperature
  • No cracking from freeze-thaw cycles
  • Maintains structural warranty for 25+ years

Lower Chemical Demands

  • Non-porous surface doesn't harbor bacteria
  • Chemical levels stay balanced longer
  • Less chlorine needed for same sanitation
  • No effect on pH like plaster has

When Gunite Still Makes Sense

To be fair, gunite pools have legitimate advantages in certain situations:

  • Pools wider than 16 feet (fiberglass limited by transport)
  • Truly custom shapes not available in fiberglass
  • Infinity edge pools requiring precise engineering
  • Pools integrated with complex architectural features
  • Estate-scale installations with unlimited maintenance budgets

An Honest Assessment

About 85% of residential pool needs can be beautifully served by fiberglass designs. The remaining 15% have specific requirements that genuinely benefit from gunite's custom capabilities. We help homeowners honestly assess which category they fall into.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the most common gunite pool problems?

Common gunite pool problems include: surface deterioration requiring replastering every 10-15 years ($10,000-$20,000), persistent algae growth in porous surfaces, cracking from freeze-thaw cycles, rough surfaces that can scrape skin, high chemical usage, and time-intensive weekly maintenance.

How much does gunite pool maintenance cost compared to fiberglass?

Gunite pools cost $1,800-$3,100 annually to maintain vs $1,300-$2,200 for fiberglass. Over 20 years, gunite pools cost $60,000-$80,000 more in maintenance including replastering. The porous surface requires more chemicals, more brushing, and more frequent professional service.

Why do gunite pools crack in Pennsylvania?

Pennsylvania experiences 100+ freeze-thaw cycles annually. Gunite's rigid structure cannot flex with ground movement and temperature changes, leading to hairline cracks. Water enters these cracks and expands when frozen, worsening damage over time. Fiberglass pools flex with temperature changes, preventing this problem.

Can I convert my gunite pool to fiberglass?

Full conversion is rarely practical or cost-effective. Options include fiberglass liners installed over existing gunite ($15,000-$25,000) or complete removal and replacement. Most homeowners with problematic gunite pools choose to replaster short-term or plan for eventual fiberglass replacement when major work is needed.

Is fiberglass or gunite better for Pennsylvania pools?

For most Pennsylvania homeowners, fiberglass is the better choice due to: resistance to freeze-thaw damage, lower lifetime maintenance costs, smoother non-porous surface that resists algae, faster installation (weeks vs months), and no replastering requirements. Gunite remains preferable only for custom shapes or very large pools.
