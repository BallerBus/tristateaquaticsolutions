How to Prepare Your Yard for Pool Installation

A smooth pool installation starts with proper preparation. Here's everything you need to do before the construction crew arrives, from clearing the site to coordinating utilities.

Congratulations on your upcoming pool installation! While your pool builder handles the heavy lifting, there are important steps you can take to ensure the project runs smoothly. Proper preparation protects your property, prevents delays, and sets the stage for a successful installation.

This guide walks through everything you need to do before excavation day.

Preparation Timeline

Here's when to tackle each preparation task:

1

4-6 Weeks Before: Planning Phase

  • Review final pool placement with builder
  • Identify trees or structures that need removal
  • Plan for outdoor furniture and equipment storage
  • Research fence contractors if needed
2

2-3 Weeks Before: Logistics

  • Schedule 811 utility marking
  • Arrange tree removal if needed
  • Plan for pet and child supervision
  • Notify neighbors of upcoming construction
3

1 Week Before: Physical Prep

  • Clear pool area of furniture, toys, decorations
  • Move potted plants to safe location
  • Ensure gate access is clear
  • Confirm driveway is available for equipment
4

Day Before: Final Check

  • Walk the access route, remove any remaining obstacles
  • Secure pets
  • Clear driveway of all vehicles
  • Confirm contact information with your builder

Utility Marking: Call 811 First

Before any excavation can begin in Pennsylvania, underground utilities must be marked. This is a legal requirement and a critical safety step.

Pennsylvania Law Requires 811

Call 811 at least 3 business days before excavation. This free service marks gas, electric, water, cable, and phone lines on your property. Failure to call can result in fines, service disruption, and dangerous situations.

What 811 Covers

  • Gas lines: Marked in yellow
  • Electric: Marked in red
  • Water: Marked in blue
  • Sewer: Marked in green
  • Cable/Phone: Marked in orange

What 811 Doesn't Cover

  • Private utilities on your property (well lines, septic, irrigation)
  • Underground dog fences
  • Low-voltage landscape lighting

Inform your builder about any private underground utilities you've installed.

Clearing the Pool Area

What Needs to Go

  • Outdoor furniture: Patio sets, umbrellas, lounge chairs
  • Play equipment: Swing sets, trampolines, sandboxes
  • Garden features: Planters, decorative rocks, birdbaths
  • Tools and equipment: Lawn mowers, wheelbarrows, hoses
  • Decorations: Lawn ornaments, flags, lighting fixtures

Define the Work Zone

Your builder will need access to more than just the pool footprint. Plan for:

  • The pool excavation area (pool size + 3-5 feet all around)
  • Equipment staging area (usually near the pool location)
  • Access route from driveway to pool site
  • Concrete/paver installation area (deck + buffer)

Clear Zone Recommendation

Plan to clear an area approximately 15-20 feet larger than your pool in each direction. This gives workers room to operate safely and store materials during construction.

Common Access Solutions

  • Fence panel removal: We can remove and reinstall fence sections
  • Driveway crossing: Ground protection mats prevent damage
  • Tree trimming: Branches can be trimmed for clearance
  • Neighbor access: Sometimes accessing through neighbor's property is easier (with permission)

What If My Access Is Tight?

Limited access doesn't necessarily mean you can't get a pool. During site evaluation, we assess access options and may recommend:

  • Smaller equipment (adds time but works in tighter spaces)
  • Alternative pool shapes that fit through available access
  • Crane-only installation from street (for over-the-house delivery)

Protecting Your Landscaping

What Will Likely Be Affected

Some lawn and landscaping impact is inevitable. Expect:

  • Lawn damage in the access path (can be reseeded/re-sodded)
  • Trampled plants near the work zone
  • Temporary damage to irrigation systems in the pool area

What You Can Protect

  • Transplant valuables: Move prized perennials before work begins
  • Flag sprinkler heads: Mark heads outside the pool area so crews can avoid them
  • Wrap tree trunks: Protect bark from equipment scrapes with burlap
  • Create buffer zones: Temporary fencing around garden beds you want protected

Trees Near the Pool

Trees can present challenges for pool installations:

  • Root systems: Can extend far beyond the tree canopy and damage pool structures
  • Debris: Overhanging branches mean more leaves, seeds, and cleanup
  • Shade: May reduce sunlight and heat on your pool

Discuss trees with your builder during design. You may want to remove some trees proactively, even if they're not directly in the pool footprint.

Communicating with Neighbors

Good neighbor relations help the project go smoothly and maintain community goodwill.

What to Share

  • Approximate start date and duration
  • Expected work hours (typically 7 AM - 5 PM)
  • Days with heavy truck traffic (delivery day, concrete pour)
  • Your contact info for questions or concerns

Common Neighbor Concerns

  • Noise: Excavation and concrete work are loud - usually 1-2 days of heavy noise
  • Dust: Digging creates dust, especially in dry weather
  • Traffic: Trucks may temporarily block the street
  • Parking: Workers need places to park

Pro Tip: A Small Gesture Goes a Long Way

A gift card for coffee or a bottle of wine with a note apologizing for the temporary disruption can build tremendous goodwill. Your neighbors will appreciate the consideration.

Day Before Installation Checklist

Final Walkthrough

  • Pool area completely cleared of all items
  • Gate access route clear of obstacles
  • Driveway clear for equipment access
  • Vehicles moved from driveway and street
  • Pets secured inside or at another location
  • Children's play area relocated if needed
  • Garden hose available for water needs
  • Exterior outlets accessible for tools
  • Utility markings visible and undisturbed
  • Builder's contact information handy

During Construction: Your Role

Once installation begins, your job is mostly to stay out of the way. However:

  • Be available: Decisions sometimes need to be made quickly
  • Keep kids and pets away: Construction sites are dangerous
  • Don't enter the work zone: Let professionals handle their space
  • Communicate concerns promptly: Don't wait until the end to raise issues

Frequently Asked Questions

What do I need to do before pool installation?

Before pool installation, you should clear the pool area of furniture and obstacles, call 811 to mark underground utilities, ensure equipment access to your backyard, remove or protect plants you want to keep, arrange for pets and children to be supervised away from the work area, and inform neighbors about upcoming construction.

Do I need to call before digging for a pool?

Yes, Pennsylvania law requires calling 811 at least 3 business days before any excavation. This free service marks the location of underground utilities (gas, electric, cable, water) to prevent dangerous strikes. Your pool builder should coordinate this, but it's ultimately the property owner's responsibility.

How wide does a gate need to be for pool installation?

For fiberglass pool delivery, equipment typically needs 10-12 feet of clearance for the crane truck. Excavators usually need 6-8 feet. If your gate is narrower, you may need to temporarily remove fence sections. Your builder will assess access during the initial site visit.

Will pool installation damage my lawn?

Some lawn damage is inevitable during pool installation due to heavy equipment and foot traffic. Professional builders take precautions like using ground protection mats, but expect to reseed or re-sod areas around the work zone. Most damage can be repaired within one growing season.

Should I remove trees before pool installation?

Trees within the pool footprint must be removed, including root systems. Trees near the pool should be evaluated - large roots can damage pool structures and future deck, and overhanging branches mean more debris in the water. Discuss tree removal with your builder during design phase.
