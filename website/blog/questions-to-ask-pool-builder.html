Questions to Ask Before Hiring a Pool Builder

The essential questions that separate quality contractors from potential problems. Protect your investment with thorough due diligence.

Hiring the wrong pool contractor can turn your dream project into a nightmare. Stories of abandoned projects, hidden costs, and shoddy workmanship are unfortunately common. The good news? Most problems are avoidable with proper vetting.

This guide provides the essential questions to ask every pool builder before signing anything. A quality contractor will answer these confidently; a problematic one will deflect or become defensive.

Licensing and Insurance Questions

These aren't optional - they're essential for your protection.

"Are you licensed as a Home Improvement Contractor in Pennsylvania?"

Pennsylvania requires HIC registration for pool installation. Unlicensed contractors have no accountability.

Good Answer:

"Yes, our HIC registration number is PA123456. You can verify it on the Attorney General's website. We're also registered in Delaware if your project is there."

"Can I see your certificate of insurance?"

Without proper insurance, YOU could be liable if a worker is injured on your property.

Good Answer:

"Absolutely. We carry $2 million in general liability and full workers' comp coverage. I'll send you a certificate naming you as an additional insured before we start."

"Who handles the electrical and plumbing work?"

These typically require licensed specialists. Using unlicensed workers can create code violations and insurance issues.

Good Answer:

"We use licensed subcontractors for all electrical and plumbing work. John Smith Electric has been our electrician for 8 years - fully licensed and insured. I can provide their credentials too."

Red Flag: "We handle everything in-house"

Unless they employ licensed electricians and plumbers on staff (rare), this may mean unlicensed work.

Experience and References

Past performance predicts future results. Dig into their track record.

"How many pools have you installed in this area?"

Local experience matters. Soil conditions, permit requirements, and climate considerations vary by region.

Good Answer:

"We've installed over 150 pools in Chester and Delaware counties over the past 10 years. I'd be happy to show you examples in your neighborhood."

"Can I visit a pool you installed 2-3 years ago?"

New pools all look good. You want to see how their work holds up over time.

Good Answer:

"Of course. I have several customers in Malvern and Wayne who are happy to show their pools. Let me arrange a visit at your convenience."

"Can you provide 5 references I can call?"

References should include a mix of recent and older projects.

Good Answer:

"Here's a list of 8 recent customers with phone numbers. Feel free to call any of them. I also have online reviews on Google and the BBB you can check."

"Have you ever had a complaint filed against you?"

This is verifiable through the BBB and PA Attorney General. How they handle problems matters.

Good Answer:

"In 15 years, we've had two complaints - both resolved to the customer's satisfaction. You can see the details and resolution on our BBB profile. No contractor is perfect, but we fix any issues."

Actually Call the References

Many homeowners skip this step. Don't. Ask references: "Would you hire them again?" and "What would you do differently?" These questions reveal more than "Were you satisfied?"

Contract and Payment Questions

The contract protects both parties. Make sure it's comprehensive and fair.

"What exactly is included in this price?"

Vague contracts lead to surprise costs. Get specifics.

Good Answer:

"Everything is itemized on page 2 of the contract: pool shell model and color, specific equipment (Pentair variable-speed pump, salt system, etc.), 500 sq ft of paver decking, fencing, permits, and electrical. Anything not listed would be a change order."

"What is your payment schedule?"

Heavy upfront payments are risky. Payments should align with work completed.

Good Answer:

"We require 20% deposit to start, 30% after excavation and shell set, 30% after equipment installation, and final 20% upon completion and your satisfaction. We don't ask for more than 50% before the pool is in the ground."

"How do you handle changes to the project?"

Change orders can balloon costs. Understand the process upfront.

Good Answer:

"Any changes require a written change order signed by both parties before work proceeds. It specifies the change, cost impact, and timeline impact. No verbal agreements - everything in writing."

"Who pulls the permits and what happens if there are issues?"

Permit problems can delay projects for weeks. Responsibility should be clear.

Good Answer:

"We handle all permit applications - it's included in your price. If there are any issues, we work with the township to resolve them. We've built in your township before, so we know their requirements."

Red Flag: "Let's just get started - we can handle permits later"

Building without permits creates serious problems: fines, removal orders, insurance voidance, and issues when selling your home.

Process and Communication

How they manage projects and communicate reveals a lot about the experience you'll have.

"What is your realistic timeline for this project?"

Overpromising is common. Get specifics and ask what could cause delays.

Good Answer:

"For a fiberglass pool this size, typically 3-4 weeks from dig to swim, weather permitting. Permits usually take 2-3 weeks before that. Rain can add a few days. I'll provide a detailed schedule once we start."

"Who will be on-site managing the project?"

You want consistent supervision, not random crews showing up without leadership.

Good Answer:

"I'm on every job site at least once daily, and my foreman Mike oversees all our projects. You'll have Mike's cell number for any questions. We don't just send crews and disappear."

"How will you communicate project updates?"

Poor communication is the #1 customer complaint about contractors.

Good Answer:

"I'll call you the night before each phase starts and text photos as we complete milestones. If anything changes, you'll hear from me immediately, not two weeks later. You can also call or text me anytime."

"What happens if something goes wrong?"

Problems happen. How they're handled matters more than whether they occur.

Warranty and After-Sale Support

The relationship doesn't end when the pool is filled. Understand what happens next.

"What warranties come with this pool?"

Get clarity on manufacturer vs. installer warranties and what's actually covered.

Good Answer:

"The pool shell has a lifetime structural warranty and 10-year surface warranty from the manufacturer. We provide a 2-year workmanship warranty on our installation. Equipment has manufacturer warranties - typically 2-3 years. I'll give you all documentation at completion."

"What maintenance will the pool need and who can service it?"

Understand ongoing requirements and whether they'll be there for support.

Good Answer:

"We'll do a full orientation when we hand over the pool - chemistry, equipment operation, seasonal care. We also offer service plans if you'd like ongoing support. But you're not locked in - any qualified pool service can work on this equipment."

Major Red Flags to Watch For

No Physical Business Address

P.O. boxes and "office in my truck" are warning signs. Legitimate contractors have real locations.

Large Cash Discount or Cash-Only Payment

This often means tax evasion and no paper trail for warranty claims or disputes.

High-Pressure Sales Tactics

"This price is only good today" or "I have another customer interested" are manipulation tactics, not legitimate offers.

Quote Without Visiting Your Property

Accurate quotes require seeing your yard. Anyone quoting from satellite images or phone descriptions will surprise you with "unforeseen" costs later.

Price Dramatically Below Competitors

If one quote is 30% cheaper, they're either cutting corners, planning change orders, or won't finish the job. Quality costs money.

Won't Provide Written Contract

Verbal agreements are unenforceable and a recipe for disputes. Everything in writing, always.

Complete Pre-Hiring Checklist

Use this checklist before signing with any pool contractor.

Documentation Verified

  • PA Home Improvement Contractor registration verified
  • Certificate of Insurance received and current
  • Workers' compensation coverage confirmed
  • Physical business address confirmed
  • Years in business verified

References Checked

  • Called at least 3 references
  • Asked "Would you hire them again?"
  • Visited at least one completed pool in person
  • Checked online reviews (Google, BBB, Yelp)
  • Verified no unresolved complaints with BBB

Contract Complete

  • Detailed scope of work included
  • All materials and specifications listed
  • Total price clearly stated
  • Payment schedule is milestone-based
  • Start and completion dates specified
  • Change order process documented
  • Warranty terms included
  • Permit responsibilities assigned

Final Gut Check

  • They answered all questions confidently
  • No pressure to sign immediately
  • They visited my property before quoting
  • I feel comfortable communicating with them
  • Their price is within normal range of other quotes

Frequently Asked Questions

What licenses does a pool builder need in Pennsylvania?

Pennsylvania requires a Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration for pool builders. Additionally, electrical work requires a licensed electrician, and plumbing may require a licensed plumber depending on municipality. Some townships require additional contractor licensing. Always verify license status with the PA Attorney General's office.

How much insurance should a pool contractor carry?

A pool contractor should carry at minimum $1 million in general liability insurance and workers' compensation coverage for all employees. Many quality contractors carry $2 million or more. Request a certificate of insurance naming you as an additional insured, and verify coverage is current.

What should be included in a pool contract?

A complete pool contract should include: detailed scope of work, all materials and specifications, total price with payment schedule, start and completion dates, permit responsibilities, change order procedures, warranty terms, and cancellation policy. Avoid contracts with vague language like "pool installation" without specifics.

How many references should I check for a pool builder?

Check at least 3-5 references, ideally including pools installed 2-3 years ago (to see how they've held up) and recent projects (to assess current quality). Ask if you can visit completed pools in person. Be wary of contractors who can't provide references or only provide family members.

What deposit is normal for pool construction?

Standard deposit is 10-33% of total project cost. Be cautious of anyone requiring more than 50% upfront. Payment should be tied to milestones: deposit to start, payment after excavation, payment after shell set, final payment upon completion. Never pay in full before work is complete.

What are red flags when hiring a pool contractor?

Red flags include: no physical business address, unlicensed or uninsured, requests large cash deposits, won't provide written contract, no references or only recent references, pushes for immediate signing with "special" pricing, won't visit your property before quoting, and quotes significantly below competitors without explanation.
