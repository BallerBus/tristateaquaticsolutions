Plunge pools have surged in popularity over the past several years, and for good reason. They offer the luxury and cooling relief of an inground pool in a fraction of the space and cost. But what exactly is a plunge pool, and is it the right choice for your home?

This guide covers everything you need to know, from the basics of plunge pool design to detailed cost breakdowns, installation timelines, and honest pros and cons.

What Exactly Is a Plunge Pool?

A plunge pool is a small, typically deep pool designed for cooling off, relaxation, and hydrotherapy rather than swimming laps. The name comes from the act of "plunging" into cool water, a practice with roots going back thousands of years.

Also known as cocktail pools, dipping pools, or spool pools (a combination of "spa" and "pool"), plunge pools typically measure:

  • Length: 10-16 feet
  • Width: 6-10 feet
  • Depth: 4-6 feet (deep enough to submerge your entire body)
  • Total footprint: 60-160 square feet

Despite their compact size, plunge pools are true inground pools. They use the same construction methods, equipment, and filtration systems as their larger counterparts. The difference is simply scale, and that smaller scale brings significant advantages in cost, maintenance, and flexibility.

Key Takeaway

A plunge pool is not a hot tub, not a spa, and not a kiddie pool. It is a properly engineered inground pool that happens to be designed for a compact space and a specific type of use: cooling off, relaxing, and entertaining in an intimate setting.

A Brief History of Plunge Pools

Plunge pools are far from a modern invention. The concept of a small, deep bathing pool has appeared across many cultures throughout history:

  • Ancient Rome: Roman bathhouses featured frigidaria, cold plunge pools used as part of a bathing ritual that alternated between hot and cold water.
  • Japan: Traditional Japanese bathing culture includes small, deep soaking pools (ofuro) designed for relaxation and hydrotherapy.
  • Australia: Modern plunge pools gained significant popularity in Australia in the early 2000s, where smaller urban lots and warm climates made them a practical alternative to full-size pools.
  • United States: The plunge pool trend reached the U.S. in the 2010s and has accelerated rapidly, especially in regions where smaller lot sizes make traditional pools impractical.

Today, plunge pools represent one of the fastest-growing segments of the residential pool industry, driven by smaller lot sizes, rising construction costs, and a growing emphasis on wellness and outdoor living.

The fundamental difference comes down to purpose. Traditional pools are designed for swimming and active recreation. Plunge pools are designed for immersion, relaxation, and creating a backyard retreat. Neither is inherently better; they serve different needs.

Who Are Plunge Pools For?

Plunge Pools Are Ideal For:

  • Homeowners with smaller lots where a full-size pool would dominate or not fit at all
  • Couples and small families who want to cool off and relax rather than swim laps
  • Wellness enthusiasts interested in cold plunging, hydrotherapy, or contrast therapy
  • Entertainers who want a sophisticated outdoor feature for hosting guests
  • Budget-conscious homeowners who want an inground pool experience at a lower cost
  • Low-maintenance seekers who want pool ownership without the demanding upkeep of a large pool
  • Urban and suburban properties in areas like the Main Line or Northern Delaware where lot sizes vary

A Plunge Pool May Not Be Right If:

You have children who need room to swim and play, you want to swim laps (without swim jets), you plan to host large pool parties with many swimmers, or you prefer a pool that doubles as a significant recreational space. In those cases, a larger pool designed for your yard may be the better investment.

Plunge Pool Sizes & Dimensions

Plunge pools come in a range of sizes, and the right one depends on your yard, budget, and how you plan to use it.

Compact Plunge Pools (10' x 6')

The smallest plunge pool option, perfect for tight urban lots or as a feature for a single person or couple. Provides enough room for 2-3 people to comfortably cool off at the same time. Typical water volume: approximately 2,000-2,500 gallons.

Mid-Size Plunge Pools (12' x 7')

The most popular size, offering a good balance between footprint and usable space. Fits 3-4 people comfortably and can accommodate a small tanning ledge or built-in bench. Typical water volume: approximately 3,000-3,500 gallons.

Large Plunge Pools (14'-16' x 8'-10')

The upper end of plunge pool sizing. These can fit 4-6 people and offer more flexibility for features like swim jets, multiple seating areas, or a combination tanning ledge and deeper section. Typical water volume: approximately 4,000-5,000 gallons.

Depth Matters

Most plunge pools feature a flat bottom at 4-5 feet deep, allowing adults to stand comfortably while being submerged to chest level. Some designs offer variable depth with a shallow tanning ledge (12-18 inches) transitioning to a deeper section (5-6 feet).

The Installation Process

Plunge pool installation follows the same general steps as a traditional pool but moves significantly faster because of the smaller scale.

Step 1: Design & Permitting (2-4 weeks)

Choose your pool model, finalize placement and features, and submit permit applications. Permitting timelines vary by municipality. In Pennsylvania, most townships require a building permit, electrical permit, and fence compliance.

Step 2: Excavation (1 day)

The smaller footprint of a plunge pool means excavation is typically completed in a single day. Access requirements are reduced compared to larger pools, making plunge pools feasible for properties with limited backyard access.

Step 3: Pool Installation (1-2 days)

For fiberglass plunge pools, the pre-manufactured shell is delivered and set into the excavated hole using a crane or excavator. The pool is leveled and backfilled.

Step 4: Plumbing, Electrical & Equipment (2-4 days)

Filtration, pump, heater (if applicable), and all plumbing and electrical connections are installed.

Step 5: Decking & Finishing (1-2 weeks)

Surrounding decking, coping, and any landscaping work is completed. This is typically the longest phase of the project.

Step 6: Fill, Startup & Inspection (1-2 days)

The pool is filled, chemicals are balanced, equipment is tested, and a final inspection is completed.

Total Timeline

From the start of excavation to first swim, most fiberglass plunge pool installations take 2-3 weeks. Including the design and permitting phase, expect 6-8 weeks from initial consultation to swimming.

Plunge Pool Maintenance

One of the biggest advantages of plunge pool ownership is reduced maintenance compared to larger pools.

Weekly Tasks (15-20 minutes)

  • Test and adjust water chemistry (pH, chlorine/sanitizer levels)
  • Empty the skimmer basket
  • Check the pump and filter operation
  • Quick visual inspection of the pool surface

Monthly Tasks

  • Clean or backwash the filter
  • Check calcium hardness and alkalinity levels
  • Inspect equipment for leaks or unusual noise

Seasonal Tasks

  • Spring opening: Remove cover, clean pool, restart equipment, balance water chemistry
  • Fall closing: Lower water level, blow out plumbing lines, add winterizing chemicals, install cover

Annual Maintenance Costs

Expect to spend $500-$1,200 per year on chemicals, filter replacements, and routine upkeep. That is roughly 40-60% less than the maintenance costs for a full-size pool, primarily because of the smaller water volume requiring fewer chemicals and less energy to filter and heat.

Pros and Cons of Plunge Pools

Advantages

  • Lower cost: $25,000-$45,000 vs $65,000-$130,000+ for a traditional pool
  • Fits small yards: Requires as little as 300 square feet of yard space
  • Faster installation: 2-3 weeks of construction vs 4-12 weeks for traditional pools
  • Lower maintenance: Less water means fewer chemicals, less energy, and less time
  • Efficient heating: Small water volume heats in hours, not days, making year-round use affordable
  • Modern aesthetic: Clean, contemporary look that enhances outdoor living spaces
  • Home value: Adds value without the potential drawback of a large pool dominating a small lot
  • Wellness benefits: Ideal for cold plunging, hydrotherapy, and relaxation

Disadvantages

  • No lap swimming: Too small for traditional swimming (though swim jets can simulate the experience)
  • Limited capacity: Typically comfortable for 2-6 people at a time
  • Not for active play: Not ideal for children who want to swim, dive, or play pool games
  • Still requires permits: Same permitting and fencing requirements as full-size pools in most municipalities
  • Resale considerations: Some buyers may prefer a full-size pool if the lot can accommodate one

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a plunge pool?

A plunge pool is a small, deep inground pool designed for cooling off, relaxation, and hydrotherapy. They typically measure 10-16 feet long, 6-10 feet wide, and 4-6 feet deep. Also called cocktail pools or dipping pools, they provide the luxury of a real pool in a compact footprint.

How much does a plunge pool cost?

Plunge pool project costs typically range from $25,000 to $45,000 including the pool shell, excavation, plumbing, electrical, basic decking, and permits. Adding features like heating, swim jets, or upgraded landscaping can bring the total to $50,000-$65,000.

What is the difference between a plunge pool and a regular pool?

Size and purpose. Traditional pools are 25-40+ feet long and designed for swimming and active recreation. Plunge pools are 10-16 feet long and designed for cooling off and relaxation. Plunge pools cost less, require less maintenance, heat faster, and fit in smaller yards.

How deep is a plunge pool?

Most plunge pools are 4-6 feet deep, allowing adults to stand comfortably while being submerged to chest level. Some designs feature a flat bottom at one depth, while others include a shallow tanning ledge transitioning to a deeper section.

Are plunge pools worth the investment?

For the right homeowner, absolutely. Plunge pools add property value, create a cooling retreat, and cost far less to install and maintain than traditional pools. They are especially valuable for smaller properties where a full-size pool is impractical.

Can you heat a plunge pool for year-round use?

Yes. Their small water volume heats quickly and efficiently. A gas heater or heat pump can bring a plunge pool to a comfortable temperature in just a few hours. Many homeowners in our region use heated plunge pools from April through October or even year-round.
