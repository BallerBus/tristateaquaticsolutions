Buying a pool is one of the most significant home improvement investments you will make. Unlike other purchases, a pool becomes a permanent part of your property and lifestyle. This guide will help you navigate the process with confidence, whether you are dreaming of summer barbecues by the water or looking for a way to add value to your home.

Understanding Pool Types

The three main types of inground pools each have distinct characteristics that affect cost, installation time, maintenance, and longevity. Understanding these differences is crucial to making the right choice for your family.

Fiberglass Pools

Fiberglass pools are pre-manufactured shells that arrive at your property ready to install. They have become increasingly popular due to their quick installation, low maintenance, and durability in freeze-thaw climates like Pennsylvania and Delaware.

  • Installation Time: 2-4 weeks
  • Lifespan: 25-30+ years (surface may need refinishing after 15-20 years)
  • Maintenance: Lowest of all pool types; smooth surface resists algae
  • Best For: Homeowners wanting low maintenance and fast installation

Concrete (Gunite/Shotcrete) Pools

Concrete pools offer unlimited customization possibilities. They are built on-site by spraying concrete over a rebar framework, then finished with plaster, pebble, or tile surfaces.

  • Installation Time: 3-6 months
  • Lifespan: 50+ years (requires resurfacing every 10-15 years)
  • Maintenance: Highest; porous surface requires more chemicals and brushing
  • Best For: Custom designs, large pools, and homeowners prioritizing aesthetics

Vinyl Liner Pools

Vinyl liner pools use a flexible liner stretched over a frame structure. While they have the lowest initial cost, liner replacement adds to long-term expenses.

  • Installation Time: 4-8 weeks
  • Lifespan: Structure lasts 25+ years; liner needs replacement every 7-12 years
  • Maintenance: Moderate; liner can be punctured and is susceptible to fading
  • Best For: Budget-conscious buyers who do not mind periodic liner replacement

Hidden Costs to Consider

  • Permits: $200 - $1,500 depending on municipality
  • HOA Approval: May require specific fencing or placement
  • Increased Insurance: $100 - $500 additional per year
  • Property Tax Increase: Varies; typically 10-15% of pool value
  • Utilities: $50 - $150/month during swimming season
  • Winterization/Opening: $300 - $600 each, annually
  • Equipment Replacement: Pump, filter, heater have 8-15 year lifespans

Pro Tip

Budget an additional 15-20% beyond the quoted pool price for landscaping, fencing, and unexpected costs. This prevents financial surprises and allows you to complete the project properly.

Installation Timeline

Understanding the installation timeline helps you plan and set realistic expectations.

Fiberglass Pool Timeline (2-4 weeks)

  1. Week 1: Excavation, pool delivery, setting shell, backfilling
  2. Week 2: Plumbing, electrical connections, equipment installation
  3. Week 3: Decking and coping installation
  4. Week 4: Final inspection, fill pool, start-up

Concrete Pool Timeline (3-6 months)

  1. Month 1: Excavation, steel installation, plumbing rough-in
  2. Month 2: Gunite/shotcrete application, curing period
  3. Month 3: Tile and coping installation
  4. Month 4: Decking installation
  5. Month 5: Interior finish (plaster/pebble), equipment
  6. Month 6: Fill, balance water, final inspection

Weather Consideration

Pennsylvania and Delaware winters can extend concrete pool timelines significantly. If you want to swim by Memorial Day, order a fiberglass pool by April or start a concrete pool the previous fall.

Choosing a Pool Builder

Your pool builder relationship will last the entire project and often beyond. Choose carefully.

Questions to Ask Every Builder

  • How long have you been installing pools in this area?
  • Can you provide references from recent projects?
  • Are you licensed and insured? Can I see documentation?
  • Do you handle permits or is that my responsibility?
  • What is your warranty on workmanship? Equipment?
  • Who will be on-site daily during construction?
  • What happens if we encounter rock or groundwater?
  • What is included in your quoted price? What is extra?
  • What is your payment schedule?
  • How do you handle change orders?

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Requiring large upfront payments (more than 10-15%)
  • No physical business address or showroom
  • Unwillingness to provide references
  • Pressure to sign immediately or "lose the deal"
  • Vague contracts without detailed specifications
  • No mention of permits or inspections
  • Significantly lower price than all competitors

Verify Licensing

In Pennsylvania, verify contractor licenses through the PA Attorney General's Home Improvement Consumer Protection Act. In Delaware, check the Delaware Division of Revenue contractor database.

Permits and Regulations

Pool installation requires permits in virtually all Pennsylvania and Delaware municipalities. Your builder should handle the permit process, but you should understand what is required.

Typical Permit Requirements

  • Building Permit: For the pool structure itself
  • Electrical Permit: For pump, lighting, and equipment
  • Plumbing Permit: Some areas require separate plumbing permits
  • Fence Permit: Safety fencing is required in PA and DE

Fencing Requirements

Both Pennsylvania and Delaware require pool fencing that:

  • Is at least 4 feet high (some areas require 5 feet)
  • Has self-closing, self-latching gates
  • Has no gaps larger than 4 inches
  • Cannot be climbed easily (no horizontal rails on outside)

For detailed local requirements, see our PA and DE Pool Regulations Guide.

Assessing Your Property

Not every backyard is ideal for a pool. Consider these factors:

Access

Can equipment reach your backyard? Fiberglass pools require a crane or large excavator to set the shell. Typical requirements:

  • Minimum 10-foot wide access path
  • No low-hanging wires or branches
  • Adequate turning radius for large trucks

Soil and Ground Conditions

  • Rock: Can significantly increase excavation costs ($5,000 - $20,000+)
  • High Water Table: Requires dewatering during construction and potentially ongoing drainage
  • Fill Soil: May need compaction or engineering

Utilities and Setbacks

  • Call 811 before any digging to locate underground utilities
  • Most areas require 5-10 foot setbacks from property lines
  • Septic systems need adequate clearance
  • Consider sun exposure for warmth and algae prevention

Popular Pool Features

While the basic pool provides swimming enjoyment, these features enhance the experience:

Essential Considerations

  • Pool Heater: Extends swimming season in PA/DE by 2-3 months ($3,000 - $6,000)
  • Salt System: Reduces chlorine handling; gentler on skin ($1,500 - $2,500)
  • LED Lighting: Color-changing lights for evening swimming ($500 - $2,000)
  • Automatic Cover: Safety, heating retention, reduced maintenance ($12,000 - $25,000)

Luxury Additions

  • Spa/Hot Tub: Year-round use, adds $15,000 - $30,000
  • Water Features: Waterfalls, bubblers, deck jets ($2,000 - $15,000)
  • Tanning Ledge: Shallow area for lounging ($2,000 - $5,000)
  • Automation System: Control everything from your phone ($2,000 - $5,000)

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Choosing solely on price: The cheapest quote often leads to the most problems
  2. Undersizing the pool: A slightly larger pool costs marginally more but offers much more enjoyment
  3. Ignoring long-term costs: Consider 20-year ownership costs, not just purchase price
  4. Skipping the site visit: Reputable builders always assess your property before quoting
  5. Not getting everything in writing: Every detail should be in the contract
  6. Forgetting about landscaping budget: The pool is just part of the backyard transformation
  7. Rushing the decision: Take time to research and compare builders
  8. Ignoring drainage: Proper drainage prevents flooding and erosion

Pool Buyer's Checklist

Before You Start

  • Determine your total budget (pool + landscaping + fencing + contingency)
  • Understand your property's limitations and opportunities
  • Research pool types and determine your preference
  • Check HOA rules if applicable
  • Verify your property survey is accurate and available

When Meeting Builders

  • Get at least three quotes from licensed builders
  • Verify licenses and insurance
  • Check references and view completed projects
  • Understand exactly what is included in each quote
  • Ask about warranty coverage and terms

Before Signing

  • Review contract thoroughly (consider legal review for large projects)
  • Confirm payment schedule is reasonable
  • Verify start date and estimated completion
  • Understand change order process
  • Get documentation of all verbal promises
