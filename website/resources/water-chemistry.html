Pool Water Chemistry Guide

Understanding water chemistry is the key to a safe, comfortable, and crystal-clear pool. Learn about pH, chlorine, alkalinity, and how these factors work together to keep your water perfect.

Pool water chemistry might seem complicated, but it follows logical principles. Master these fundamentals, and you will spend less time fighting problems and more time enjoying your pool. This guide explains each chemical factor, how they interact, and how to maintain optimal balance.

The Key Chemical Parameters

Five main factors determine your water quality. Understanding each one is essential for proper pool care.

pH Level

7.2 - 7.6

Measures acidity/alkalinity on a scale of 0-14. Low pH (acidic) corrodes equipment and irritates skin. High pH reduces chlorine effectiveness and causes cloudy water.

Free Chlorine

1 - 3 ppm

Your primary sanitizer that kills bacteria and algae. "Free" chlorine is the active, working chlorine available to sanitize. Combined chlorine has already reacted and needs to be eliminated.

Total Alkalinity

80 - 120 ppm

Acts as a buffer for pH, preventing rapid pH swings. Think of it as pH's "shock absorber." Always balance alkalinity before adjusting pH.

Calcium Hardness

200 - 400 ppm

Measures dissolved calcium in water. Too low and water becomes corrosive, damaging surfaces and equipment. Too high causes scaling and cloudy water.

Cyanuric Acid (CYA)

30 - 50 ppm

Stabilizer that protects chlorine from UV degradation. Without it, sunlight destroys chlorine rapidly. Too much reduces chlorine's effectiveness.

Understanding pH

pH is the most frequently tested and adjusted chemical factor. It measures how acidic or basic your water is on a scale of 0 (very acidic) to 14 (very basic), with 7 being neutral.

pH Scale for Pools

6.8 Acidic 7.2 7.4 Ideal 7.6 8.0 Basic

Why pH Range Matters

  • Below 7.2: Water becomes corrosive, damaging metal equipment, heaters, and surfaces. Can cause eye and skin irritation.
  • 7.2 - 7.6: Optimal range. Chlorine works effectively, water is comfortable, equipment is protected.
  • Above 7.6: Chlorine becomes less effective (at 8.0, only 20% of chlorine is active). Causes cloudy water, scaling, and skin irritation.

Adjusting pH

Problem Solution Product
pH too high Add acid to lower pH Muriatic acid or sodium bisulfate (pH Down)
pH too low Add base to raise pH Sodium carbonate (soda ash/pH Up)

pH Adjustment Rule

Always adjust in small increments. Add chemicals, run pump for 4-6 hours, then retest. It is easier to add more than to correct an overshoot.

Chlorine: Your Primary Sanitizer

Chlorine kills bacteria, viruses, and algae to keep your pool safe. Understanding the different forms of chlorine helps you choose the right products.

Types of Chlorine

  • Free Chlorine: Active chlorine available to sanitize. This is what you measure and maintain at 1-3 ppm.
  • Combined Chlorine: Chlorine that has reacted with contaminants (chloramines). Causes "chlorine smell" and eye irritation. Should be below 0.5 ppm.
  • Total Chlorine: Free + Combined. If total is significantly higher than free, you have a chloramine problem.

Chlorine Products

Product Form Best Use Notes
Trichlor Tablets Slow-dissolving tabs Daily chlorination Contains stabilizer; adds CYA over time
Dichlor Granules Fast-dissolving granules Quick boost, spas Contains stabilizer; pH neutral
Cal-Hypo Granules or tablets Shocking No stabilizer; adds calcium; raises pH
Liquid Chlorine Liquid (sodium hypochlorite) Daily/shocking No stabilizer; raises pH; short shelf life

Shocking Your Pool

Shocking means adding a large dose of chlorine to:

  • Destroy chloramines (combined chlorine)
  • Kill algae and bacteria that regular chlorine cannot handle
  • Restore water clarity after heavy use or contamination

Shock at Night

Always shock your pool in the evening. UV light rapidly degrades chlorine, so daytime shocking wastes most of the product. Evening application allows chlorine to work through the night.

Total Alkalinity: The pH Buffer

Total alkalinity (TA) measures the water's ability to resist pH changes. Think of it as a shock absorber for pH - proper alkalinity keeps pH stable.

How Alkalinity Works

  • Too Low (under 80 ppm): pH bounces around, difficult to control. Water can become corrosive.
  • Proper Range (80-120 ppm): pH remains stable. Chemical additions have predictable effects.
  • Too High (over 120 ppm): pH tends to drift high. Can cause cloudy water and scaling.

Balance Order Matters

Always balance Total Alkalinity BEFORE adjusting pH. Changes to alkalinity affect pH, but changes to pH have minimal effect on alkalinity. Adjust alkalinity first, wait, then fine-tune pH.

Calcium Hardness

Calcium hardness measures dissolved calcium in your water. All water needs some calcium - too little or too much causes problems.

Effects of Improper Calcium

  • Too Low: Water becomes "aggressive" and will dissolve calcium from plaster surfaces and concrete, causing etching and damage.
  • Too High: Calcium precipitates out, causing scaling on surfaces, equipment, and waterline.

For fiberglass pools: Target the lower end of the range (200-250 ppm) since there is no plaster to protect.

For concrete/plaster pools: Target mid-range (250-350 ppm) to protect the surface.

Water Testing Methods

Test Strips

Quick, easy, no measuring, good for frequent testing

Less precise, can be affected by old strips, higher cost per test

Liquid Test Kits

More accurate, lower cost per test, tests more parameters

Takes more time, requires careful measurement, reagents expire

Digital Testers

Very accurate, instant readings, great for pH/TDS

Higher initial cost, requires calibration, limited parameters

Professional Analysis

Most comprehensive, tests everything, includes recommendations

Takes time to get results, requires bringing sample to store

Testing Schedule

Parameter Testing Frequency Ideal Range
pH 2-3 times per week 7.2 - 7.6
Free Chlorine 2-3 times per week 1 - 3 ppm
Total Alkalinity Weekly 80 - 120 ppm
Calcium Hardness Monthly 200 - 400 ppm
Cyanuric Acid Monthly 30 - 50 ppm
Professional Analysis 2-3 times per season Comprehensive

Chemical Addition Safety

  • Never mix chemicals - especially chlorine and acid. This creates toxic gas.
  • Add chemicals to water - never add water to chemicals.
  • Store chemicals separately - in cool, dry, well-ventilated area.
  • Pre-dissolve granular chemicals - in a bucket of pool water before adding to pool.
  • Add chemicals with pump running - for proper distribution.
  • Wait between additions - at least 4-6 hours between different chemical types.
